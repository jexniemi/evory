## How the Raise Calculator Works

A salary raise calculator helps you quickly determine your new pay after a percentage increase. Enter your current annual salary and the raise percentage, and the tool instantly shows your new annual salary, the annual dollar increase, and how much more you will take home each month. This is useful for evaluating job offers, negotiating a raise, planning a budget, or comparing compensation packages.

### Raise Calculation Formula

> **New Salary = Current Salary × (1 + Raise% ÷ 100)**
>
> **Annual Increase = New Salary − Current Salary**
>
> **Monthly Increase = Annual Increase ÷ 12**

### Worked Example

You currently earn **$62,000 per year** and receive a **4.5% raise**:

- New Annual Salary: $62,000 × 1.045 = **$64,790**
- Annual Increase: $64,790 − $62,000 = **$2,790**
- Monthly Increase: $2,790 ÷ 12 = **$232.50/month**

A 4.5% raise may sound modest, but it adds over $230 to every monthly paycheck — that compounds into significant purchasing power over a career.

## Understanding Salary Raises

### Average Raise Percentages

Knowing what a typical raise looks like helps you benchmark your own situation. According to compensation surveys, average annual raises in the United States typically fall between 3% and 5% in stable economic conditions, while high-performing employees may receive 6–10% or more.

### The Long-Term Value of a Raise

One of the most important aspects of a salary raise is compounding. Each raise builds on your existing base salary, which means raises early in your career have a disproportionately large impact over time. Consider two employees starting at $50,000:

- **Employee A** receives a 3% raise every year
- **Employee B** negotiates 6% raises every year

After 20 years, Employee A earns approximately **$87,700**, while Employee B earns approximately **$153,600** — nearly double. The difference of just 3 percentage points per year compounds into a massive lifetime earnings gap.

### How to Negotiate a Raise

Negotiating effectively requires preparation and confidence. Start by researching market rates for your role using tools like Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, or the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Know your value: document achievements, quantify impact, and be ready to articulate why you deserve more than the standard increase.

Timing matters — ask during performance reviews, after successfully completing a major project, or when you have a competing offer. Be specific with your ask: instead of "I'd like a raise," say "Based on my contributions and market data, I am requesting a 7% increase."

### Raises vs. Cost of Living

A raise that falls below the inflation rate is effectively a pay cut in real terms. If inflation is 4% and you receive a 3% raise, your purchasing power has actually declined. Use the [Inflation Calculator](/apps/inflation-calculator) to understand how inflation affects your real salary over time.

### Tax Implications of a Raise

A higher salary means a higher gross income, but your take-home increase will be somewhat less due to federal and state income taxes and payroll taxes. The marginal tax rate on additional income depends on your bracket. Use the [Tax Bracket Calculator](/apps/tax-bracket-calculator) to see exactly how much of your raise you'll keep after taxes.

### Raise vs. Job Change

Studies consistently show that switching jobs results in larger salary increases than staying at the same employer. The median salary jump from changing jobs is 10–20%, while the average internal raise is 3–5%. However, job changes carry additional costs and risks — new learning curve, loss of seniority, benefits changes, and uncertainty.
