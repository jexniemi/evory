### Why the U.S. Uses Imperial Units

The United States is one of only three countries (along with Myanmar and Liberia) that has not officially adopted the metric system as its primary measurement system. While the metric system was legalized for trade in 1866, widespread adoption never occurred due to the massive cost of switching infrastructure, road signs, product labels, and consumer habits. Congress passed the **Metric Conversion Act of 1975**, but it was voluntary and had limited impact.

In practice, many American industries already use metric: science, medicine, pharmaceuticals, the military, and automotive manufacturing. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) actually defines U.S. customary units in terms of metric equivalents — the inch is officially defined as exactly 25.4 millimeters.

### Quick Conversion Tips

- **Miles to km:** Multiply by 1.6 (or use the Fibonacci sequence — each number is approximately the km equivalent of the previous number in miles: 5 mi ≈ 8 km, 8 mi ≈ 13 km)
- **Feet to meters:** Divide by 3.28, or multiply by 0.3
- **Inches to cm:** Multiply by 2.54 (exact)
- **Cm to inches:** Divide by 2.54, or multiply by 0.394
