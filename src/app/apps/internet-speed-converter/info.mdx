## Internet Speed Converter — Mbps, MB/s, Gbps Explained

Internet speed is confusing because ISPs advertise in megabits per second (Mbps) while downloads show megabytes per second (MB/s). These are not the same — there are 8 bits in a byte, so 100 Mbps is only 12.5 MB/s. This converter helps you translate between all common internet speed units and see real-world download times.

## Mbps vs MB/s — The Key Difference

> **1 byte = 8 bits**  
> **Mbps ÷ 8 = MB/s**

**Example:** Your ISP advertises 200 Mbps:
- Actual download speed: 200 ÷ 8 = **25 MB/s**
- Time to download a 4 GB movie: 4,000 MB ÷ 25 = **160 seconds** (about 2.7 minutes)

## What Speed Do You Need?

- **1–5 Mbps**: Basic email and web browsing
- **5–25 Mbps**: SD video streaming, video calls
- **25–100 Mbps**: HD streaming, gaming, small household
- **100–300 Mbps**: 4K streaming, multiple users, remote work
- **300+ Mbps**: Large households, heavy downloading, streaming on many devices
- **1+ Gbps**: Power users, content creators, home servers

## Why Your Actual Speed Is Lower Than Advertised

ISPs advertise "up to" speeds which represent the theoretical maximum. Actual speeds are usually 50–80% of the advertised rate due to:

- **Network congestion**: More users on the same node slows everyone down, especially during peak hours (7–11 PM).
- **Wi-Fi overhead**: Wi-Fi is slower than wired Ethernet due to signal interference, distance from router, and protocol overhead.
- **Hardware limits**: Older routers, outdated network cards, or long Ethernet cables reduce throughput.
- **Throttling**: Some ISPs throttle specific traffic types (streaming, torrenting) during congestion.
