## How the Paycheck Calculator Works

The paycheck calculator estimates your take-home pay by subtracting federal income tax, state income tax, FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare), and voluntary deductions like 401(k) contributions from your gross pay. The result is an approximation — actual withholding depends on your W-4 elections, filing status, and pre-tax benefits.

### Paycheck Calculation Formula

> **Net Pay = Gross Pay − Federal Tax − State Tax − FICA − Deductions**

For a biweekly pay schedule (26 pay periods per year):

> **Biweekly Gross = Annual Salary ÷ 26**

**Worked Example:** An employee earning $65,000/year with a 22% federal rate, 5% state rate, and 6% 401(k):

- Biweekly gross: $65,000 ÷ 26 = **$2,500.00**
- 401(k): $2,500 × 6% = **$150.00**
- Taxable income: $2,500 − $150 = **$2,350.00**
- Federal tax: $2,350 × 22% = **$517.00**
- State tax: $2,350 × 5% = **$117.50**
- FICA: $2,500 × 7.65% = **$191.25**
- Net pay: $2,500 − $517 − $117.50 − $191.25 − $150 = **$1,524.25**

## Understanding Your Pay Stub

Every paycheck stub contains several key sections. Here is what each line means:

### Gross Pay
Your total earnings before any deductions. For salaried employees this is your annual salary divided by the number of pay periods. Hourly workers multiply hours worked by their hourly rate.

### Federal Income Tax
Withheld based on IRS tax brackets, your filing status, and the allowances on your W-4. The US uses a progressive tax system — only income in each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate.

### FICA Taxes
FICA stands for the Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It funds Social Security (6.2% on the first $168,600 of earnings in 2024) and Medicare (1.45% on all earnings, plus an additional 0.9% on earnings above $200,000 for single filers).

### State Income Tax
Varies dramatically by state. Nine states — Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming — have **no state income tax**. California's top rate is 13.3%, while most states fall between 3% and 6%.

## Tips to Maximize Your Take-Home Pay

1. **Optimize your W-4** — Claiming the right number of allowances prevents over-withholding (giving the IRS a free loan).
2. **Max out pre-tax deductions** — 401(k) contributions and HSA/FSA reduce your taxable income.
3. **Use tax credits** — Credits like the Earned Income Tax Credit reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar.
4. **Consider a Roth conversion** — If you expect higher future tax rates, a Roth 401(k) may yield better after-tax income in retirement.
