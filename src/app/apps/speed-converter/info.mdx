### Why Different Speed Units Exist

- **mph** — Used in the United States, United Kingdom, and a few other countries that retained Imperial measurements for road speed. All US speed limits and car speedometers display mph
- **km/h** — The standard in most of the world. Used for road signs, weather reports, and everyday purposes across Europe, Asia, South America, and Africa
- **m/s** — The SI base unit for speed. Used universally in science, physics, and engineering. Wind speeds in weather forecasts are sometimes given in m/s
- **Knots** — The nautical mile per hour. One knot = one nautical mile (1.852 km) per hour. Used universally in aviation and maritime navigation because nautical miles relate directly to degrees of latitude

### Understanding Knots in Aviation and Sailing

The knot is the international standard for aviation and marine speed. Pilots report airspeed, ground speed, and wind speed in knots. Air traffic control, weather reports (METARs and TAFs), and nautical charts all use knots.

**Why?** One nautical mile equals one minute of latitude, making navigation calculations simpler. At 60 knots, a vessel covers 1 degree of latitude per hour.

### The Mach Number

For very high speeds, the **Mach number** expresses speed relative to the speed of sound:

- **Mach 1** = speed of sound ≈ 767 mph (1,235 km/h) at sea level
- **Mach 2** = twice the speed of sound ≈ 1,534 mph
- Commercial jets fly at about **Mach 0.80–0.85** (500–575 mph)
- The SR-71 Blackbird reached **Mach 3.3** (2,200 mph)
- The Space Shuttle re-entered the atmosphere at **Mach 25** (17,500 mph)
